Abstract 

Classical proportional–integral–derivative (PID) control is widely employed in industrial applications; however, achieving higher performance often mo-tivates the adoption of model predictive control (MPC). Although gradient-based methods are the standard for real-time optimization, sampling-based approaches have recently gained attention. In particular, model predictive path integral (MPPI) control enables gradient-free optimization and accom-modates non-differentiable models and objective functions. However, directly sampling control input sequences may yield discontinuous inputs and increase the optimization dimensionality in proportion to the prediction horizon. This study proposes MPPI–PID control, which applies MPPI to optimize PID gains at each control step, thereby replacing direct high-dimensional input-sequence optimization with low-dimensional gain-space optimization. This formulation enhances sample efficiency and yields smoother inputs via the PID structure. We also provide theoretical insights, including an information-theoretic interpretation that unifies MPPI and MPPI–PID, an analysis of the effect of optimization dimensionality on sample efficiency, and a character-ization of input continuity induced by the PID structure. The proposed method is evaluated on the learning-based path following of a mini forklift using a residual-learning dynamics model that integrates a physical model with a neural network. System identification is performed with real driving data. Numerical path-following experiments demonstrate that MPPI–PID

1. Introduction 

In practical control development, classical proportional–integral–derivative (PID) control remains the established standard. However, to achieve control performance beyond that of PID control, model predictive control (MPC) [1] has been actively studied. As computational methods for real-time optimiza-tion, gradient-based methods are standard, yet sampling-based approaches have recently attracted attention. Model predictive path integral (MPPI) control [2], a representative method in the latter category, provides a highly flexible framework for computing control inputs without gradient calcula-tions, even when the plant model or objective function is non-differentiable. This property enables MPPI to handle plant models based on complex non-linear machine-learning approaches, such as neural networks [3], as well as control specifications with objective functions involving logical branching [4], providing substantial practical benefits. Conversely, as MPPI determines control input based on random samples, the resulting input often exhibits discontinuities. Furthermore, because MPPI directly optimizes the input se-quence, it does not retain the standard PID-controller architecture that is widely adopted in industrial practice, which can complicate deployment and tuning. In addition, (as with standard MPC) the dimensionality of the opti-mization variables scales with the horizon length, which imposes a substantial computational burden. To realize MPC-based real-time optimization while preserving the struc-ture and continuity of PID control, approaches have been proposed that treat PID gains—rather than the control input itself—as optimization vari-ables and perform gradient-based real-time optimization [5, 6, 7, 8]. Various 2studies [5, 6, 7] have targeted linear systems, while others [8] have targeted nonlinear systems based on physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) [3]. However, gradient-based optimization methods are often challenging to em-ploy when the plant model or objective function is non-differentiable and require computationally expensive gradient calculations. Therefore, in this study, we propose a method that updates PID gains— treated as optimization variables—at each control step via sampling-based, model-based optimization using MPPI. The objective is to preserve the struc-ture and continuity of PID control while ensuring versatility (for handling complex control objectives) and computational efficiency. Specifically, the expected benefits are as follows. 

• Handling complex control objectives: As the optimization is based on MPPI, the method can be applied even when the plant model or ob-jective function is non-differentiable. 

• Continuity of the control input: As the PID control law has a filter structure based on the integral and derivative of the error, the control input tends to be smoother than in conventional MPPI, which directly samples the control input. 

• Computational efficiency: In sampling-based optimization using MPPI, computationally expensive gradient calculations are unnecessary. More-over, while in conventional MPPI the dimensionality of the optimiza-tion variables scales with the horizon length, in the proposed method, the dimensionality of the optimization variables equals the (small) num-ber of PID gains, so improved sample efficiency is expected. 

• Compatibility with existing PID controllers: By optimizing only the PID gains while preserving the PID control-law structure, the method can enhance performance without redesigning or replacing existing PID controllers commonly used in industrial applications. Furthermore, we provide theoretical insights that elucidate the proposed method. Specifically, we (i) present an information-theoretic interpretation of an update rule that unifies conventional MPPI with the proposed method, 3(ii) analyze the influence of the optimization dimension on sample efficiency through the effective sample size, and (iii) analyze the continuity of the con-trol input arising from the PID structure. Among related methods, PI 2 (policy improvement with path integrals) [9] is a path-integral-type reinforcement learning approach closely related to MPPI. PI 2, which has also been applied to the tuning of PID gains, is a model-free method that optimizes control policy parameters on an episode-by-episode basis [10]. In contrast, the proposed method employs model-based, receding-horizon optimization at each control step, thereby enabling online adaptation of the PID gains in response to predicted future tracking cost under the current operating condition. In this study, we apply the proposed control method to a residual-learning model that integrates a physical model and a neural network (Fig. 1). In

3. Results 

This section presents system identification based on empirical data col-lected from a mini forklift and evaluates state prediction performance. Sub-sequently, it reports comparative results for fixed-gain PID control, conven-tional MPPI, and the proposed method (MPPI–PID) in path-following con-trol. 

3.1. Dataset construction 

In this study, we construct a time-series dataset comprising the state and control input xt = [ Xt, Y t, s t, c t, v Xt , v Yt , r t]T , ut = [ at, δ t]T , derived from manual real-machine driving data of the mini forklift system introduced in [17]. The original dataset contains [Xt, Y t, ψ t, v Xt , v Yt , r t, a t, δ t]T ; however, in 16 this work, we use st = sin( ψt), c t = cos( ψt) to avoid the discontinuity of the yaw angle ψ.The data were recorded at a sampling rate of approximately 15 Hz ( h ≃

1/15 ≃ 0.0667 s), yielding a total of 70,926 samples (corresponding to ap-proximately 78.8 minutes). Due to missing intervals in the data, the log was segmented at points where the time difference exceeds 0.1 s. Subsequently, for each continuous interval, the data were resampled via linear interpolation to yield an equally spaced time series ( h = 0 .0667 s), and then applied a median filter and a moving-average filter. The filter window sizes were set for each signal as follows. 

• X, Y, ψ : median window 3, moving-average window 9 

• vX , v Y , r : median window 3, moving-average window 7 

• a, δ : median window 3, moving-average window 5 Finally, to align the basic unit used in training and evaluation, each pre-processed continuous interval was uniformly partitioned into 5.0 s segments (approximately 75 steps). These fixed-length segments were subsequently split into training, validation, and test datasets in a 70:15:15 ratio.

The identification parameters ka, k V , k δ, k r of the physical model were es-timated via least squares using the full training dataset. For both the stan-dard neural network and the residual-learning model, the state and control input were normalized by division with their per-dimension standard devia-tion. 4. Discussion 

From Table 2, the residual-learning model attains the highest average R2.In particular, the velocities (vX , v Y ) exhibit marked improvement relative to the physics-based model, indicating that the residual neural network corrects nonlinear dynamics that are not fully captured by the physics model. The position (X, Y ) and attitude (s, c ) already achieve high values at the physics-model stage, yet the residual-learning model slightly improves them further. An exception arises in the yaw rate r, where the standard neural network model performs best. As position and speed errors directly influence control performance in path-following control, the residual-learning model, which achieved the highest overall accuracy, is adopted for control. The proposed method suppresses control-input increments compared with conventional MPPI, while preserving tracking performance. This is consis-tent with the theoretical analysis on the continuity of the control input: because the optimization variables are limited to the PID gains θ, and the control input sequence is computed through the PID structure incorporat-ing the integral and derivative of the errors, the temporal changes of a

and δ become smoother than in conventional MPPI. Moreover, the low-sample results support the anticipated improvement in sample efficiency. In conventional MPPI, the dimensionality of the optimization variables is 

nuN = 2 × 60 = 120 , whereas in the proposed method it is 9, correspond-ing to the number of PID gains. Consequently, the ability of the proposed 23 Fig. 5: Time series of states, control inputs, path-following error, and control-input incre-ments obtained in path-following control ( I = 16 ) (fixed-gain PID control / conventional MPPI / proposed method (MPPI–PID)) 

24 method to sustain effective path-following performance even with I = 16 is consistent with the effective-sample-size analysis presented in the theoretical section. Although fixed-gain PID control yields smooth control inputs, the increased path deviation highlights its limitation in tracking performance for the reference path. 

5. Conclusions 

In this study, we introduce a receding-horizon PID gain optimization method based on MPPI (MPPI–PID). We establish theoretical insights that unify conventional MPPI and MPPI–PID within an information-theoretic framework, and demonstrate, through effective-sample-size and temporal-correlation analyses, the sample-efficiency and input-smoothness advantages of the proposed approach. The method is applied to learning-based forklift path-following control using a residual-learning dynamics model trained on real-machine data. Numerical results show improved tracking performance compared with fixed-gain PID control and smoother inputs than conventional MPPI, while retaining favorable performance under substantially reduced sample counts. Future work includes experimental validation on a real machine, extension to stochastic control under model uncertainty, and integration with obstacle-avoidance strategies.
