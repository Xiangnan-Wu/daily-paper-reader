Abstract. Vision-Language Navigation (VLN) requires an agent to nav-igate through 3D environments by following natural language instruc-tions. While recent Video Large Language Models (Video-LLMs) have largely advanced VLN, they remain highly susceptible to State Drift in long scenarios. In these cases, the agent’s internal state drifts away from the true task execution state, leading to aimless wandering and failure to execute essential maneuvers in the instruction. We attribute this failure to two distinct cognitive deficits: Progress Drift , where the agent fails to distinguish completed sub-goals from remaining ones, and Memory Drift , where the agent’s history representations degrade, making it lose track of visited landmarks. In this paper, we propose a Dual-Anchoring Framework that explicitly anchors the instruction progress and history representations. First, to address progress drift, we introduce Instruc-tion Progress Anchoring, which supervises the agent to generate struc-tured text tokens that delineate completed versus remaining sub-goals. Second, to mitigate memory drift, we propose Memory Landmark An-choring, which utilizes a Landmark-Centric World Model to retrospec-tively predict object-centric embeddings extracted by the Segment Any-thing Model, compelling the agent to explicitly verify past observations and preserve distinct representations of visited landmarks. Facilitating this framework, we curate two extensive datasets: 3.6 million samples with explicit progress descriptions, and 937k grounded landmark data for retrospective verification. Extensive experiments in both simulation and real-world environments demonstrate the superiority of our method, achieving a 15.2% improvement in Success Rate and a remarkable 24.7% gain on long-horizon trajectories. To facilitate further research, we will release our code, data generation pipelines, and the collected datasets. 

Keywords: Vision-Language Navigation · Video-LLMs · World Model

## 1 Introduction 

Vision-Language Navigation (VLN) has emerged as a central challenge in em-bodied artificial intelligence [14,27], requiring an agent to follow natural language instructions to navigate in a previously unseen environment. Due to its profound potential for real-world applications in domestic service [15, 44], intelligent per-sonal assistants [36, 37], etc, this task has garnered significant attention and witnessed rapid development in recent years. Most VLN works follow a perception-to-action pipeline: the agent conditions on egocentric observations and the instruction to predict an action, iterating un-til reaching the goal. While effective for short instructions, this stepwise policy is brittle under long, compositional instructions. As navigation proceeds, agents may exhibit severe State Drift : their task state becomes progressively unreli-able, and the agent gradually loses a consistent sense of (i) where it is in the instruction and (ii) where it has been in the environment. In other words, the internal state increasingly drifts away from the true task state, leading to erratic behaviors. As illustrated in Fig. 1, we attribute State Drift to two coupled fail-ure modes: (1) Progress Drift , where instruction stage is mis-tracked and the boundary between completed and remaining sub-goals becomes blurred; and (2) 

Memory Drift , where history representations degrade over long trajectories, causing the agent to lose track of visited landmarks. Recent Video-LLMs [17, 30] have advanced VLN [13, 31, 35, 43, 50] by lever-aging powerful pre-trained representations. However, they remain prone to state drift in long-horizon scenarios. Fundamentally, these models rely on next-action prediction, a result-oriented objective that enforces correct local moves but ne-Abbreviated paper title 3

glects the agent’s internal cognitive process. This supervision allows the agent to “guess" the right action based on shallow local cues. However, without explicit constraints on the internal states, they will inevitably decouple from physical reality as navigation proceeds. To address these challenges, we argue that robust navigation needs explicit state anchoring for instruction progress and visual history. For the former, we use structured language as a compact representation of instruction execution sta-tus. Instead of relying on implicit attention to infer progress, the agent explicitly maintains a “mental checklist”, structurally delineating completed sub-goals from remaining ones. For the latter, we introduce a retrospective constraint: robust state grounding requires not only forward-looking control but also explicit verifi-cation of past observations. We therefore compel the model to preserve distinct, landmark-centric history representations to firmly anchor its current position. In this paper, we propose the Dual-Anchoring Framework, which regularizes the Video-LLM’s internal state through two complementary branches. First, to explicitly track instruction progress, we introduce Instruction Progress An-choring . By designing a Progress Annotation Generation pipeline, we curate 3.6 million navigation samples where each action is prefixed with a structured progress description, explicitly delineating completed versus remaining sub-goals. Then, we utilize Instruction-Aware Co-training to force the model to articulate a concrete linguistic state before executing any control. Second, to enforce histor-ical verification, we propose Memory Landmark Anchoring . To realize this, we devise a Landmark Frame Mining method to collect 937k grounded land-mark data. Then, we design a Landmark-Centric World Model that operates retrospectively: it reconstructs the high-level semantic features of the most re-cently passed landmark from output tokens of history frames. This module acts as a "rear-view mirror," compelling the model to preserve distinct, grounded representations of its history. Our main contributions are summarized as follows: 

– We identify State Drift as a critical bottleneck in VLN and propose the Dual-Anchoring Framework, a unified solution that explicitly regularizes the agent’s internal state, greatly eliminating erratic behaviors. 

– We introduce Instruction Progress Anchoring, backed by a scalable pipeline that synthesizes 3.6 million structured samples to enforce explicit instruction state tracking. Complementarily, we design a Memory Landmark Anchoring that collects 937k grounded landmark data and leverages SAM-based retro-spective prediction to anchor the agent’s visual memory. 

– We conduct extensive experiments on VLN-CE benchmarks, where our method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance. Furthermore, real-world de-ployment demonstrates the framework’s superior robustness and generaliza-tion in complex, unseen environments. 4 K. Wu et al. 

## 2 Related Works 

2.1 Vision Language Navigation. 

Vision-Language Navigation in Continuous Environments (VLN-CE) [25] re-quires agents to interpret instructions and execute low-level actions in physics-realistic 3D spaces. Unlike graph-based settings [10,11,19,53], VLN-CE presents significant challenges in aligning continuous perception with long-term planning. The emergence of Video Large Language Models (VideoLLM) [4,5,17,30] has bi-furcated recent solutions into Hierarchical Planners and End-to-End Navigators. Hierarchical methods [13] decouple reasoning and control, treating the VLM as a "slow system" for mid-level plans. Conversely, End-to-End models [43,50] fine-tune Video-LLMs to map streaming observations directly to actions, leveraging the base model’s strong generalization. However, both approaches remain sus-ceptible to state drift during extended trajectories. Without explicit state man-agement, the agent’s internal representation tends to decouple from the physical reality. In this work, we mitigate this drift by enforcing explicit regularization on the decision process: we supervise the agent to articulate structured progress descriptions for semantic alignment, and leverage an object-centric objective to predict historical features for visual grounding. 

2.2 Multi-task Training for VLN. 

Standard VLN training relies on human-annotated navigation datasets like R2R [3] and RxR [26]. However, the scarcity of such high-quality trajectory data limits the agent’s ability to generalize across diverse unseen environments. To address this, the community has embraced Multi-task Training, broadly categorized into two paradigms. The first focuses on Broad Visual Generalization, where agents are co-trained on massive synthetic environments [13] or real-world hu-man videos [12] to master diverse visual distributions. The second emphasizes Reasoning Alignment, employing auxiliary tasks like Hierarchical Planning [31] or Chain-of-Thought (CoT) generation [21] to rationalize the perception-action loop. However, these auxiliary tasks remain predominantly action-centric or in-stantaneous, focusing primarily on interpreting the current scene for immediate decision-making. Consequently, they often neglect the explicit alignment be-tween the accumulated history and the instruction, leaving agents vulnerable to Progress Drift. Our Instruction Progress Anchoring shifts focus from local perception to global status tracking, strictly enforcing a structural distinction between “Done” and “Remaining” parts to lock the decision process. Similar auxiliary supervision strategies have been explored more broadly in multimodal learning, aiming to stabilize optimization and improve robustness beyond direct end-task prediction [28, 29, 32, 33, 45, 46]. 

2.3 World Model for VLN. 

World models serve as internal representations of the environment, enabling agents to perform mental simulation by predicting future states. Early approaches, Abbreviated paper title 5

such as NWM [6] and AstraNav-World [20], operate as Generative Simula-tors, synthesizing high-fidelity future video streams to learn general physical priors. However, pixel-level generation is computationally prohibited for real-time onboard inference. To address this efficiency bottleneck, recent works like LS-NWM [52], NavMorph [47], and NavForesee [31] shift towards Feature-level Prediction, forecasting compact latent dynamics or semantic features instead of raw images. However, these methods remain fundamentally future-centric. They prioritize foresight—anticipating what will happen next—while neglecting the explicit maintenance of what has already happened. In long trajectories, this lack of historical grounding leaves agents vulnerable to Memory Drift, where the history representation fades. Diverging from this foresight-dominant paradigm, we propose a Landmark-Centric World Model that prioritizes hindsight. Instead of hallucinating uncertain futures, we leverage the Segment Anything Model to reconstruct the object-centric features of previously visited landmarks. This ret-rospective objective compels the agent to anchor its internal state to the concrete visual history, effectively mitigating perceptual aliasing.

3.2 Instruction Progress Anchoring 

To mitigate Progress Drift, the agent must explicitly maintain a mental checklist of its execution status. However, standard VLN datasets only provide coarse-grained trajectory-level instructions, lacking step-by-step state annotations. To bridge this gap, we introduce an automated pipeline to synthesize fine-grained progress description data at scale. 

Progress Annotation Generation. We leverage a powerful Multimodal LLM ( Qwen3-VL) as an offline annotator to generate textual progress summaries yprog t

given a ground-truth trajectory τ = {(ot, a t)}Tt=0 and its instruction I. As illus-trated in Fig. 3, the pipeline operates in four sequential steps. 1) Visual Kinemat-ics Prompting: To bridge the gap between static frames and dynamic navigation, we explicitly overlay the frame index and executed action text (e.g., “Turn Left 15 ◦”) onto the top-left corner of each image frame. This explicitly informs the annotator of the agent’s kinematics between timestamps. 2) Interval Sampling: 

We perform interval sampling along the trajectory with a stride of n. For each sampled step t, the annotator receives the instruction I and the processed visual history sequence o0: t. 3) Dual-Step Reasoning: To ensure high-quality annota-tion, we prompt the model to perform a chain-of-thought process: first analyzing the visual evidence against the instruction , and then generating a concise sum-mary of Completed Sub-goals using the original wording. 4) Instruction-Aligned Refinement: Finally, to eliminate intermediate reasoning and possible hallucina-tions, we prompt the LLM to distill the analysis into a single, concise sentence. This step strictly constrains the output to be a verbatim prefix of the instruc-tion. In this way, we collect a large-scale dataset comprising 3.6 million progress description samples. 

Instruction-Aware Co-training. During training, we treat this as a co-training task, where the ground-truth action is prefixed with the synthesized progress descriptions. In this case, we add “find out which part you have com-pleted” in the Prompt. The generation objective at step t is modified to maximize 

P (yprog t , a t|I , Ht), forcing the agent to articulate its status before action.

3.4 Data Composition and Training Strategy 

To ensure both the robustness and generalizability of our model, we compile a comprehensive training set combining standard navigation benchmarks, our synthesized anchoring data, and general vision-language data. Abbreviated paper title 9

Data Composition. As summarized in Table 1, our training mixture consists of four main categories. The Base Navigation data includes 180K trajectories from R2R, RxR, and EnvDrop, alongside 155K ones from a ScaleVLN subset. To explicitly enforce state anchoring, we augment these trajectories with our self-collected State Anchoring data, comprising 3.6M progress descriptions and 937K grounded landmark SAM features. Finally, the dataset is supplemented with 240K DAgger rollouts for robust policy learning, as well as 400K VideoQA pairs and 230K image-text pairs to preserve general vision-language capabilities.

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate our proposed method on two continuous VLN benchmarks: R2R-CE [25] and RxR-CE [26]. R2R-CE is based on the Matterport3D simulator and contains 90 scenes with 5.6k tra-jectories. RxR-CE is a larger dataset with more complex paths and fine-grained instructions, which is particularly suitable for evaluating the agent’s ability to handle long-horizon navigation and instruction following. We report standard metrics for VLN-CE: Success Rate ( SR ), Success weighted by Path Length (SPL ), Oracle Success Rate ( OSR ), and Navigation Error ( NE ). 

Implementation Details. Our model is built upon StreamVLN [43] with LLaVA-Video [51] as the backbone. We use the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-5. All experiments are conducted on 32 NVIDIA H200 GPUs. Detailed dataset statistics and training hyperparameters are provided in the Appendix.

## A Implementation Details 

A.1 Training Details 

Our model is implemented based on the LLaVA-Video [2] architecture, utilizing Qwen-1.5 as the large language model and SigLIP-2 (SO400M-Patch14-384) as the vision encoder. To capture multi-scale visual details, we extract features from the penultimate layer (Layer -2) of the vision encoder and process them using an any-resolution grid configuration (supporting up to 9 grids) before passing them through an MLP2x_GELU projector. The training process is conducted on 32 NVIDIA H200 GPUs. To ensure efficient memory utilization and stable scaling across multiple nodes, we employ DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 optimization, Gradient Checkpointing, and BF16 mixed pre-cision with TF32 enabled. For the temporal dimension, we uniformly sample 32 frames from the streaming video and strictly append 8 history frames to maintain the context, supporting a maximum sequence length of up to 32,768 tokens. For our proposed Dual-Anchoring mechanism, both the Instruction Progress Anchoring (formulated as Chain-of-Thought intermediate steps) and the Mem-ory Landmark Anchoring (formulated as the World Model retrospective predic-tion) are activated with a 50% probability during the training batches. Specifi-cally, the Landmark-Centric World Model leverages the last-layer hidden states of the language model to reconstruct the target SAM feature map, which has a spatial dimension of 256 × 64 × 64 . A.2 DAgger Algorithm Details 

To address the distribution shift between training (teacher-forcing) and inference (student-forcing) in continuous environments, we employ a modified Dataset Aggregation (DAgger) algorithm for robust data collection. Our implementation details are as follows: 

– Rollout Strategy: We execute the policy trained in Stage 1 across the training environments. To fully expose the model to its own induced state distribution, we set the mixed policy probability to 0, meaning the agent relies entirely on its own visual observations and historical context to predict actions autoregressively without initial expert guidance. 

– Expert Intervention: Instead of a simple distance threshold, we utilize a dynamic intervention mechanism monitored by a shortest-path expert. The expert forcefully takes over control if any of the following critical errors occur: (1) The model outputs an empty action sequence; (2) The model predicts a STOP action prematurely while the distance to the goal is ≥ 3.0 meters; (3) The accumulated action errors exceed a predefined tolerance threshold, indicating severe deviation from the reference path. Once triggered, the ex-pert executes optimal actions to guide the agent back to the next reference waypoint before returning control to the student model. 

– Data Filtering and Aggregation: To ensure the high quality and ped-agogical value of the collected dataset, we apply strict filtering criteria. An episode is only aggregated if the agent successfully reaches the vicinity of the goal (distance < 0.5 meters). Furthermore, it must meet one of the follow-ing efficiency criteria: (1) For corrected trajectories (where the expert inter-vened), the relative path length (PL) must be < 0.93 ; (2) For autonomous trajectories (no expert intervention), the PL must be < 0.85 . – Co-Training Strategy: In Stage 2, we integrate the newly collected DAg-ger samples (approximately 240K) into a unified co-training dataset. Action Mapping and Low-Level Control. Upon receiving the streaming observations, the remote model processes them to predict the next discrete nav-igational action in real-time. The controller converts the target pose into continuous velocity commands ( Twist messages) by publishing to the /cmd_vel_bridge topic. Kd = 0 .0, capping the maximum linear velocity at 0.6 m/s. For yaw (rotation) control, we set Kp = 1 .5 and Kd = 0 .0, with a maximum angular velocity of 0.5 rad/s. 

System Performance and Data Collection. The entire control loop operates reliably at a frequency of 10 Hz (100 ms cycle time). This low-level setup achieves high-precision execution, maintaining a position error of < 0.1 m and an angular error of < 0.1 rad, which limits the overall trajectory tracking error to less than 5%. Concurrently, a data collection node securely records the synchronized RGB images, global pose matrices, action commands, and timestamps to the local directory for offline analysis and evaluation. ## B Additional Experiments 

B.1 Evaluation of Generated Progress 

To evaluate the quality of the generated progress during inference and its di-rect impact on downstream tasks, we conduct additional experiments on the R2R-CE [1] dataset. The evaluation is formulated as follows: at any given time step t, the model is provided with the history sequence o0: t alongside the natu-ral language instruction I, and is tasked with describing the current execution progress. We compare our approach against two references: a Baseline model trained without our Instruction Progress Anchoring, and the offline Progress Annotation Generation (PAG) pipeline (utilizing Qwen3-VL) detailed in Sec. 3.2, which serves as the theoretical upper bound for this task. Following our data assessment protocol, we initially employ an LLM-as-a-Judge to calculate two automated metrics: Hallucination Rate (HR) and Logical Consistency Score (LCS). However, while these reference-free metrics effectively penalize fabricated entities and verify structural contiguity, they cannot defini-tively determine whether the generated progress accurately grounds to the true physical state. To address this, we conducted a supplementary user study to eval-uate User Study Correctness (USC), where 10 AI researchers provided a strict binary judgment ( 1 or 0) on whether the generated progress matches the visual history o0: t. Furthermore, to reveal the correlation between internal progress tracking and final task execution, we concurrently report the downstream navi-gation metrics: Success Rate (SR) and Success weighted by Path Length (SPL). As shown in Table S2, the Baseline model struggles significantly to track its status, exhibiting a high Hallucination Rate (19.2%) and failing to maintain logical contiguity (LCS of 1.45). Most strikingly, its extremely low USC score of 29.3% indicates that without explicit anchoring, the model’s internal state almost completely decouples from physical reality. This severe cognitive deficit directly translates to inferior downstream execution, yielding a navigation SR of only 56.9% and an SPL of 51.9%. In contrast, our proposed method effectively mitigates these deficits, drastically reducing the HR to 7.62% and boosting the USC to 86.7%, closely approaching the offline PAG upper bound (USC 93.2%). Crucially, this massive improvement in absolute physical grounding directly cor-relates with superior navigation performance, boosting the SR to 65.6% and SPL to 62.1%. These aligned results compellingly validate that accurate progress ver-ification is a fundamental prerequisite for successful long-horizon navigation.
