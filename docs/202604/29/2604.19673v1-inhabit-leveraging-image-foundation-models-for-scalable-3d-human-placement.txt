## 1 Introduction 

Understanding 3D scenes from a human-centric perspective is essential for robotics and embodied AI [28, 34, 40]. Beyond geometric reconstruction and semantic la-beling, this requires perceiving how humans would naturally interact with a scene, whether that is cooking at a stove, sitting on a sofa, or pausing at a window to look outside. However, progress is bottlenecked by a data scarcity problem. While we do have large collections of static 3D scenes [8, 29], and sep-arately large collections of 3D human motions captured in studios [25], there are no large datasets of 3D humans meaningfully interacting with diverse 3D environments. Capturing such interactions with motion capture inside natural scenes is logistically prohibitive and scales poorly. The handful of existing cap-tured datasets [15, 16, 18] cover fewer than 30 scenes each. Synthetic generation offers an alternative, but existing approaches rely on geometric heuristics that optimize for collision avoidance and contacts without deeper consideration of what a person would be doing in a given scene [17, 38]. Meanwhile, 2D foundation models trained on internet-scale data have implicitly acquired rich commonsense knowledge of how humans interact with their en-vironments [23]. Image-editing models [5, 12] can synthesize what it looks like to cook at a stove or read on a couch, because they have seen millions of such images and vision–language models [1, 12] can process the image of a room and reason about what activities the scene affords. We introduce InHabit, a fully automated data-generation engine that brings the 2D capabilities of foundation models into the 3D domain for synthesizing large-scale 3D human–scene interaction data, as follows. InHabit follows a ren-der–generate–lift principle: we render an existing 3D scene and using a VLM [12] we propose contextually meaningful actions tailored to the visible environment. Leveraging an image foundation model [12] we then generate one or more hu-mans performing these actions into the rendered view. Finally, we lift the humans back to the 3D scene with an optimization-based method inspired by PhySIC [36] which reconstructs physically plausible SMPL-X [27] bodies within the known 3D scene geometry. This process requires no manual annotation and is scalable. Our method outperforms GenZI [24] and POSA [17] on quantitative placement metrics and is preferred in 78% of cases in a perceptual user study. To show scalability, we apply InHabit to the Habitat-Matterport 3D dataset [29], producing the first large-scale photorealistic human–scene interaction dataset, Inhabit 3

which we call InHabitants . It contains over 78k samples across ∼800 building-scale scenes, with complete 3D geometry, SMPL-X bodies, and RGB images. The dataset includes both single-person and multi-person interactions. We demonstrate the practical value of the InHabitants data on two down-stream tasks. First, augmenting standard training data with our samples im-proves the SOTA contact estimation method DECO [31] on the DAMON bench-mark [31]. This is remarkable as DECO required labor intensive contact anno-tations, whereas our data can be generated fully automatically. Second, existing feed-forward HSI reconstruction models [7,37] consistently improve when trained using our data, achieving stronger performance on PROX and RICH benchmarks than when trained on their original data alone. In summary, our contributions are: 1. A fully automated, scalable method for generating semantically meaningful human–scene interactions in 3D scenes, combining VLM-based and genera-tive image affordance reasoning, and optimization-based 3D reconstruction. 2. The first large-scale 3D HSI dataset with over 78k samples across ∼800 building-scale scenes, including complete scene geometry, SMPL-X humans performing a wide variety of interactions, and RGB images. 3. Quantitative evidence that our synthetic data improves both contact esti-mation and feed-forward HSI reconstruction, and a perceptual user study in which our interactions are strongly preferred over existing methods. We will release the dataset and code publicly. 

## 2 Related Works 

2.1 Human Placement in 3D Scenes 

The dominant approach to placing humans in 3D scenes relies on optimization over geometric constraints such as contact and penetration [16, 38]. POSA [17] goes further by learning data-driven contact priors from captured interactions. These methods are effective at producing physically plausible configurations, but not necessarily semantically plausible ones. A body can be placed standing at any flat surface, yet there is no reasoning about what exactly one should be doing, or where one’s attention should be directed. Alternatively, [24] gener-ates 3D human-scene interactions using natural language description but also requires the user to provide a coarse point location in the 3D scene where the interaction should occur. Our method addresses this limitation by leveraging the implicit interaction priors of 2D foundation models to place humans in a scene-appropriate way, without human in the loop. 

Leveraging 2D Image Generation for 3D Tasks. A recent line of work uses 2D image or video generation models as a source of training signal for 3D tasks. HumanWild [11] generates humans in diverse scenes using diffusion models and recovers 3D annotations from the generated images, showing that 2D genera-tion can substitute for expensive real-world capture. Beyond the Contact [21] 4 N. Kister et al. 

uses 2D inpainting diffusion to synthesize images of humans interacting with objects, then lifts these to 3D affordance maps, showing that interaction knowl-edge latent in 2D models can be extracted and grounded in 3D. DAViD [20] generates 2D human–object interaction videos using video diffusion and lifts them to 4D interaction samples. The same paradigm of using 2D generative models to produce annotated 3D human data has been explored for pose esti-mation [22]. These works establish the feasibility of using 2D generative models as a source of 3D human data, but they operate on isolated human–object pairs or single tasks without reasoning about the broader scene context, and none produce full building-scale HSI data. InHabit extends this paradigm to complete indoor environments: a VLM reasons about what interactions a scene affords, an image-editing model visualizes them in context, and an optimization-based reconstructor grounds the result in the full 3D scene geometry. 

2.2 Human–Scene Interaction Datasets 

Early datasets such as PiGraphs [30] and PROX [16] established the paradigm of paired human–scene data using RGB-D recordings, but are limited to a handful of scenes. Subsequent work [14, 15, 18, 39] improved capture quality with precise motion capture inside scanned scenes, yet the dependency on controlled MoCap infrastructure limits their scale to at most 30 scenes. TRUMANS [19] reaches 100 scenes by procedurally rearranging objects, but remains MoCap-dependent for human poses and is semantically constrained. Synthetic datasets [4, 6, 35] offer scale but place prerecorded human motions into scenes without reasoning about what interactions those scenes afford, yielding humans that are physically valid in the scene but are not engaging with it. CIRCLE [2] combines VR with MoCap to capture richer behaviors, but contains only 9 scenes. Therefore, the current tradeoff is that either the data is semantically grounded but small, or it is large but lacks semantic richness. By using InHabit, we introduce our InHabitants 

dataset which provides the best of both worlds. It contain over 78k photorealistic samples across ∼800 building-scale scenes, where every interaction is tailored to the specific semantic context by 2D foundation models and lifted to physically plausible 3D annotations. 

## 3 Method 

Our goal is to generate large-scale 3D human–scene interaction (HSI) data with natural, diverse, and context-aware behaviors. Existing 3D scene collections are relatively abundant, but they typically contain no people and therefore lack interaction supervision for embodied scene understanding. We formulate data generation as a render–generate–lift loop (Fig. 2): we render an existing 3D scene, use image foundation models to generate realistic humans in image space, and lift the results back into physically grounded 3D bodies aligned with the known scene geometry. Given any static 3D indoor scene (e.g., large-scale scanned environments such as HM3D [29]), InHabit has four stages. (1) We automatically sample camera viewpoints, render RGB/depth views, and perform scene-aware human insertion in image space (Sec. 3.1), where foundation models generate interaction pro-posals and edited images. (2) We reconstruct and place inserted humans in 3D with geometric and physical constraints from the full scene mesh (Sec. 3.2). (3) We scale this process to large corpora through automated multi-view generation and batching (Sec. 3.3). (4) Because each stage can introduce artifacts, we apply post-reconstruction quality control to retain only physically plausible samples (Sec. 3.4). The final output is a dataset of RGB-D images paired with complete 3D scene geometry and aligned SMPL-X human meshes. 

3.1 Generating Scene-aware Humans in 2D 

The goal of this stage is to populate rendered scene views with humans perform-ing diverse, contextually meaningful interactions. This requires two complemen-tary capabilities: understanding what interactions a scene affords, and knowing 

how those interactions look when realized visually. We address both through the interplay of two foundation models—a vision–language model (VLM) and an image-editing model—whose combined knowledge of human behavior in indoor environments drives our insertion pipeline. A natural starting point is to simply instruct the editing model with a generic prompt such as “Add a person to this image.” In practice, however, this yields mostly generic standing poses that ignore the rich affordances visible in the scene—a kitchen with a stove affords cooking, a living room with a sofa facing a TV affords watching, yet none of this is captured by a generic instruction. Hand-crafting scene-specific prompts ( e.g ., “Add a person sitting on the couch 6 N. Kister et al. 

watching TV”) would require per-scene annotations or a human in the loop, neither of which scales. Instead, we query the VLM to analyze each rendered view and propose plau-sible activities given the visible scene context. The VLM’s world knowledge lets it reason jointly about objects, their functions, and typical human behaviors— recognizing, for instance, that a kitchen with a microwave affords operating it, or that a bedroom affords lying down. We refer to this as affordance-driven prompting : it produces interactions that are semantically appropriate for each scene without any predefined text prompts or manual supervision. However, affordance-driven proposals tend to converge on the most common activities and miss rarer but plausible ones. We therefore complement it with an action-driven 

strategy: given a compact set of predefined actions ( e.g ., standing, reaching, leaning), we ask the VLM to contextualize each within the current scene. For example, the generic action “standing” in a room with a ceiling light may be-come “standing on a chair to replace the light bulb.” Starting from actions rather than affordances unlocks a substantially wider range of interactions from a small vocabulary. Since InHabit inserts humans into a fixed 3D scene, interactions that alter the scene state ( e.g ., opening a door, moving furniture) or introduce new ob-jects would break consistency with the known geometry. We therefore explicitly constrain the VLM to propose only interactions that preserve the scene layout; residual violations are caught by our post-reconstruction filtering (Sec. 3.4). The framework also naturally extends to multi-person scenarios—we prompt for in-teractions involving multiple people ( e.g ., two people conversing in a hallway, a group seated around a dining table), and the editing model inserts them with coherent spatial relationships and social context, something difficult to achieve with optimization-based placement methods [17, 24, 38] that operate per-person without modeling interpersonal coordination. Given the VLM’s textual interaction proposals, the image-editing model serves as the visual realization engine. Crucially, it does not simply paste a human template into the image—it generates body poses, proportions, and spa-tial placements that are adapted to the local scene context. A person watching TV is posed seated and facing the screen; a person cooking is oriented toward the stove with arms extended (Fig. 1). These fine-grained, scene-adapted pose variations emerge naturally from the editing model’s large-scale visual pretrain-ing and would be difficult to reproduce with optimization-based methods that rely on restricted pose priors without visual scene understanding (Fig. 5). The output of this stage is the rendered scene image with one or more photorealis-tic humans inserted, ready for 3D lifting (Sec. 3.2). Full prompt templates and additional design details are provided in the supplementary material. 

3.2 Lifting Inserted Humans to 3D 

Our lifting framework is inspired by prior work on physically-grounded human-scene reconstruction, such as PhySIC [36] and PROX [16]. PhySIC directly es-timates 3D scenes from RGB images by jointly optimizing predicted human Inhabit 7

meshes and partial depth maps, whereas PROX fits humans to RGB-D frames using physical constraints. In contrast, we leverage known, complete 3D scene geometry, which removes one degree of freedom from optimization and con-strains the problem more tightly, resulting in more accurate human placement and alignment with scene geometry. At the same time, our method retains the flexibility of image-based approaches, as it does not rely on RGB-D inputs like PROX. This enables us to generate accurate 3D data at scale, leveraging image foundational models. Crucially, access to the complete scene mesh means the scene SDF is globally well-defined, making penetration and contact terms stable and informative. It also improves occlusion handling, since body parts not visible in the RGB view can still be constrained by nearby scene surfaces. We provide a detailed abla-tion comparing full-scene versus partial-geometry lifting in the supplementary material. Specifically, we estimate a monocular depth map of the inserted human using a depth estimator [33] and back-project it to 3D to obtain a partial human point cloud. This point cloud is then aligned with known 3D scene geometry to initialize the human localization. Unlike PhySIC, which co-optimizes scene geometry and can implicitly absorb metric inconsistencies, we operate with fixed scene geometry and introduce an explicit scale parameter s applied to the human mesh in camera-relative space. This parameter controls the human’s metric size and apparent scale in the scene. Without s, the optimizer would need to adjust body shape β and translation t

to match the scene scale; however, modifying β alters the body silhouette and is penalized by the 2D reprojection loss Lproj , preventing reliable recovery of the correct scale. The explicit scale parameter decouples metric sizing from body shape, improving convergence and physical consistency. Additional analysis is provided in the supplementary material. We optimize the human parameters — pose θ, shape β, camera-relative translation t, and scale s — with respect to the following objective:

This diversity exposes the generation pipeline to kitchens, bedrooms, offices, hallways, stairwells, and many other room types, enabling a broad distribution of human–scene interactions in the resulting dataset (Fig. 3). Generating high-quality data at this scale requires careful viewpoint selec-tion. Not every camera pose yields a view that is suitable for human insertion: some look directly at a nearby wall or are obstructed by furniture, while others show only ceilings or narrow corridors where inserting a full-body human is infea-sible. We therefore filter the available viewpoints using a depth-based heuristic. Specifically, we require that (i) the floor is visible in the lower portion of the im-age, so that inserted humans can be grounded rather than truncated, and (ii) the view extends sufficiently deep into the room, so that the scene context is rich enough for meaningful interactions. Both criteria are evaluated by checking the rendered depth map against simple thresholds; views that fail either check are discarded. Details of the heuristic are provided in the supplementary material. Regardless of the underlying scene source, some rooms will inevitably be largely empty and offer limited affordances for human–scene interaction ( e.g ., bare corridors, unfurnished storage rooms). driven prompting (Sec. 3.1) naturally produces only a narrow set of generic activ-ities. To maintain pose diversity even in such scenes, we augment the prompting strategy with textual pose descriptions sourced from the PoseScript dataset [9], which provides fine-grained natural-language descriptions of human body poses. By conditioning the editing model on these descriptions, we can inject varied body articulations that go beyond what affordance- or action-driven prompting alone would produce, ensuring that the dataset covers a broad pose distribution even in visually sparse environments. To characterize the resulting dataset, we use a VLM [13] to classify each generated sample by its action and the primary object involved, in an open-ended manner. Volume Filter. The image editing model can occasionally insert children, which fall outside the adult body model we employ (SMPL-X), and the human detec-tor can misfire on depictions of people in portraits, posters, or sculptures. In both cases, fitting an adult SMPL-X mesh to these detections produces highly deformed body shapes: either unnaturally compressed to match a child’s pro-portions or distorted to conform to a non-human surface. We observe that these failure modes consistently yield meshes with anomalously low body volume. Mo-tivated by this, we compute the volume V(Hi) of each reconstructed SMPL-X mesh Hi and discard any instance with V(Hi) < τ vol . Despite its simplicity, this filter is highly effective: it removes the vast majority of problematic cases and, in practice, performs comparably to more expensive alternatives such as dedi-cated human detectors (e.g., Multi-HMR [3]) or pose-prior-based filtering (e.g., VPoser [27]). Together, these three filters ensure that the final dataset contains only hu-mans that are sufficiently visible, properly aligned, plausibly shaped, and do not introduce implausible scene changes. 

## 4 Experiments 

We evaluate InHabit by comparing against existing SOTA human placement methods both quantitatively and qualitatively. To demonstrate the quality of Inhabit 11 

our InHabitants data, we show how it can be used to improve the performance of a SOTA contact estimation method. 

4.1 Experimental Setup 

We compare against the state of the art on the task of text-based HSI-Generation. 

Baselines. GenZI [24] is a zero-shot human–scene interaction method that combines Stable Diffusion inpainting with 2D-to-3D lifting. Given a text prompt and a target location, it renders the scene from multiple views, inpaints a human in each view, and recovers SMPL-X parameters via multi-view optimization. Generation is performed in two stages: four candidate poses are produced, and the best is refined in a second stage. 

POSA [17] is an optimization-based method that predicts per-vertex contacts and semantic classes using a trained VAE, and then optimizes body pose and placement to align predicted contacts with scene geometry and semantics (lim-ited to 40 object categories). As POSA does not support text-based generation, we extend it with text-driven pose retrieval to form POSA++. Specifically, we use PoseEmbroider [10] to retrieve poses from text via a joint embedding space. Since PoseEmbroider requires PoseScript-style descriptions [9], we employ an LLM to convert our HSI prompts accordingly. We use AGORA poses [26], on which POSA’s contact prediction performs best. 

Data. We use scenes from HM3D [28] to compare InHabit against the baselines. As POSA requires semantic labels for the 3D scene, so we restrict ourself to the subset of 200 scenes from HM3D. We sample 150 of our generated affordance-aware text prompts and corresponding scene locations. We do furthest point sampling with respect to sentence embeddings to create a diverse set of interac-tions. 

Metrics. We adopt the CLIP score, as in [24], to evaluate whether placements are semantically consistent with the input prompt. For each pose, we render ten views of the generated human mesh using a fixed human texture for realism, following GenZI. We compute the CLIP score for each view with respect to the input text and report the average. To evaluate the physical plausibility and semantic alignment of generated contacts, we introduce a set of contact metrics focused on specific interaction using interaction-contact priors. For a given text prompt we use a LLM to predict the body parts expected to be involved in the interaction (e.g. "feet", "hands" and "arms" for leaning on a counter ) We define a body surface vertex as being in contact if its distance to the nearest scene surface falls below a predefined threshold. Using our LLM-derived labels, we calculate Recall to measure the proportion of expected contact vertices successfully recovered, and Precision to quantify the presence of false-positive contacts in unrelated body regions. These are summarized via the F1-score. Additionally, we report the contact distance which is the average distance of the body vertices that should be in contact for 12 N. Kister et al. 4.3 Quantitative Results Contact Estimation on DAMON. To scale contact supervision, we extract contact labels directly from our reconstructed humans by using spatial proximity between body vertices and scene surfaces. This yields large-scale pseudo-labels without additional manual annotation. In total, our dataset provides 97K hu-mans, i.e., 97K image-to-contact pairs. More importantly, it provides substan-tially higher scene diversity: unlike video-based datasets with many redundant frames, our data spans roughly 800 building-scale scenes with significantly more distinct rooms and layouts, see Fig. 4. We train DECO [31] on different dataset combinations and report perfor-mance on the DAMON benchmark in Table 1. The results show that training with DAMON+Ours greatly outperforms DECO trained with DAMON alone. It also outperforms training on DAMON+TRUMANS [19]. This is remarkable, as TRUMANS is a labor-intensive real-world motion capture dataset in instru-mented scenes, whereas our dataset is generated automatically. One reason is that TRUMANS is limited to 100 scenes, offers less action diversity, and contains images that are less realistic than ours. InHabit covers around 800 building-scale scenes with much broader environmental diversity. 

Human-Scene-Reconstruction on PROX and RICH. We train two recent HSI–reconstruction methods Human3r [7] and GRAFT [37] on our generated dataset and evaluate on PROX Quantitative [16] and RICH [18]. Human3r was designed for video input so we adapt it to train on individual images. Human3r trained on our data outperforms the original which was trained on [4] on PROX in terms of human pose metrics and exceeds it by a large margin on the scene contact metrics (F1-score and V2S), see Table. 2. The reason is that Human3r was trained on BEDLAM which lacks the realistic interactions of our dataset.

## 5 Conclusion 

We have presented InHabit, a fully automatic data generator for large-scale 3D human–scene interactions by transferring the implicit interaction knowledge of 2D foundation models into 3D. Using a render–generate–lift principle, we popu-lated approximately 800 building-scale scenes from Habitat-Matterport 3D with over 78k contextually meaningful SMPL-X humans, producing InHabitants , the first large-scale 3D HSI dataset with complete scene geometry, body meshes, and RGB images. We showed that InHabit outperforms existing placement meth-ods both quantitatively and in a perceptual user study, and that augmenting training data with InHabitants improves state-of-the-art contact estimation and feed-forward HSI reconstruction on established benchmarks. Currently, InHabit generates static poses within static 3D scenes, but the principle can be extended to video generation and dynamic scenes in the future as the underlying genera-tive models continue to evolve. More broadly, our results show that internet-scale 2D foundation models encode enough knowledge of human–environment interac-Inhabit 15 

tions to serve as a practical, scalable alternative to expensive 3D motion capture for training embodied AI methods.
