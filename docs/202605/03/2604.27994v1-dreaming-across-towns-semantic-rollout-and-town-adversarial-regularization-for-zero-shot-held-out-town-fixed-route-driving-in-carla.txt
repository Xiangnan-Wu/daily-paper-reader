# Dreaming Across Towns: Semantic Rollout and Town-Adversarial Regularization for Zero-Shot Held-Out-Town Fixed-Route Driving in CARLA 

Feeza Khan Khanzada, Student Member, IEEE, and Jaerock Kwon, Member, IEEE 

Abstract —Learned driving agents often degrade when deployed in unseen environments. This paper studies a deliberately bounded instance of that problem in the CARLA simulator: zero-shot transfer of a closed-loop fixed-route driving agent from Town05 and Town06 to unseen Town03 and Town04. The study isolates struc-tural town shift by keeping weather fixed to ClearNoon and removing traffic and pedestrians. We build on a Dreamer-style latent world-model agent and add two training-only auxiliary losses: multi-horizon prediction of future visual-semantic embeddings along imagined rollouts and town-adversarial supervision on a semantic projection of the recurrent latent state. A causal context feature conditions the semantic rollout predictor, while the actor and critic retain the standard control feature. The policy receives no navigation command, route polyline, goal pose, or map input; the reference route is used only by the environment for reward, progress, success, and termination. Across the evaluated held-out towns, the proposed model achieves the highest mean success rate among the included Dreamer-family methods. Secondary safety and lane-keeping metrics are mixed across towns. These results support a bounded conclusion: in this controlled fixed-weather CARLA setting, semantic rollout supervision combined with town-adversarial regularization improves mean held-out-town route completion. 

Index Terms —Autonomous driving, route following, model-based reinforcement learning, world models, domain generalization, domain-adversarial learning, semantic representation learning, CLIP, CARLA. 

I. Introduction 

Learned driving agents often degrade when deployed in environments that differ from those seen during training. This paper studies a deliberately bounded instance of that problem in the CARLA simulator [1] i.e zero-shot held-out-town transfer for route-unconditioned fixed-route closed-loop driving. Training uses only Town05 and Town06, and evaluation is performed without target-domain adaptation on unseen Town03 and Town04 under fixed ClearNoon weather and without traffic or pedestrians. The scope is deliberately restricted. Training is per-formed on source towns only, and evaluation is performed in held-out towns without target-domain adaptation. The goal is therefore not to claim broad robustness to all forms of autonomous-driving distribution shift, but to test whether a learned route-following agent can retain route-relevant competence when town layout and lane topology change. Existing strategies only partially address this problem. Image augmentation can improve invariance to low-level appearance changes, but it does not explicitly regular-ize imagined future representations. We do not claim robustness to weather, traffic, pedestrians, sensor cor-ruption, or real-world deployment. Instead, we evaluate whether these training-time auxiliaries improve held-out-town transfer in a fixed-weather CARLA route-following setting using task-completion, safety, lane-keeping, and speed metrics. II. Related Work 

This section reviews prior work most relevant to the present study: learning-based autonomous driving, world-model-based driving, domain-invariant representation learn-ing, structured representations for transfer, vision-language supervision, and CARLA evaluation protocols. 

A. Learning-based Autonomous Driving Policies 

End-to-end driving policies learn a direct mapping from observations to control, often with additional conditioning or structure to mitigate ambiguity. Conditional Imitation Learning (CIL) conditions policies on high-level commands to reduce multimodality in demonstrations and improve controllability across maneuvers [5]. Learning by Cheating (LbC) leverages privileged information available in simula-tion to train a privileged “teacher” and distill behavior into a vision-based student, aiming to inherit robustness while deploying only standard sensory inputs [6]. Related efforts seek to reduce reliance on dense labels by learning compact visual abstractions that support driving control with less annotation burden [7]. In parallel, RL-based driving has been explored as an alternative to imitation when rewards and interaction are available, including recent end-to-end RL formulations in urban driving settings [8]. Hybrids that combine imitation with reinforcement learning objectives have also been proposed to exploit demonstration priors while still improving through interaction [9]. These works primarily improve the policy mapping from perception to control, using supervision, RL, or hybrid objectives. In contrast, our paper studies how latent structure inside a world-model agent affects zero-shot transfer under held-out-town shift. 

B. World Models and Latent Planning for Driving 

World models aim to learn a compact latent dynamics representation that enables planning, imagination, or model-based value learning from high-dimensional observa-tions. Early latent dynamics approaches for planning from pixels established the core idea: learn a probabilistic latent transition model and plan in latent space [10]. Dreamer-style agents operationalize this by training a world model and optimizing behavior via imagined rollouts, enabling model-based RL without explicit online planning at every step [11]. DreamerV3 scales this paradigm across diverse domains through architectural and optimization refine-ments, strengthening the case for general-purpose world-model agents [3]. In autonomous driving, Think2Drive [12] formulates CARLA-v2 driving as model-based reinforce-ment learning with a latent world model used as a neural simulator for planning, illustrating the relevance of latent imagination for driving control. Within autonomous driving, multiple works explore how to instantiate world-model ideas under the constraints of closed-loop control, safety-critical dynamics, and rich visual scenes. Learning to Drive from a World on Rails uses learned predictive structure to support driving-relevant reasoning under constrained dynamics and perception [13]. MILE jointly learns a world model and a driving policy for urban driving and reports transfer to unseen towns and weather, making it a close driving-specific precedent for world-model-based transfer [14]. SEM2 likewise ar-gues for filtering world-model features toward driving-relevant semantics through a semantic masked recurrent world model [15]. More recent driving-focused world-model systems argue for aligning learned dynamics with driving semantics and controllable factors; for example, Raw2Drive formulates end-to-end autonomous driving in a model-based RL framework and emphasizes aligned world models for sample efficiency and performance under varying conditions [16]. Prior driving world-model papers mainly emphasize predictive fidelity, planning efficiency, or control perfor-mance. Our paper instead studies whether semantic rollout regularization and town-adversarial regularization on a semantic projection improve zero-shot transfer under held-out-town shift within a DreamerV3-style route-following agent. 

C. Domain Shift, Domain Randomization, and Adversarial Invariance 

Robustness to domain shift is a central theme in learning-based robotics. Domain randomization popularized the strategy of varying textures, lighting, and other simulator parameters during training to encourage invariance and transfer [17]. Dynamics randomization extends this idea to physical parameters, showing that broadening the range of simulated dynamics can improve transfer of control policies [18]. When explicit randomization is insufficient or expensive, adaptation mechanisms aim to infer latent environment parameters online. Rapid Motor Adaptation (RMA) learns to adapt behavior via a learned latent “context” inferred from recent history, supporting fast adaptation to changing dynamics [19]. Another family of approaches targets domain shift by enforcing domain invariance in learned representations. Domain-adversarial training uses a gradient reversal layer to learn features predictive for the task but uninformative about the domain [20]. Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) formalizes the goal of learning predictors that remain optimal across environments, motivating invariance as a principle for out-of-distribution generalization [21]. For RL specifically, domain adaptation has been studied through shared representations or unified latent dynamics across domains, aiming to transfer behaviors learned in one environment to another [22]. 3

Our setting is domain generalization within simulation under held-out-town shift. Rather than broadening the training distribution through randomization, we retain a fixed training distribution and apply domain-adversarial supervision to a semantic projection inside a DreamerV3-style world model. A history-conditioned context feature conditions the semantic rollout predictor, while the actor and critic retain the standard Dreamer feature. Relative to prior adversarial and invariance-based methods, the contribution here is the placement of these constraints inside a driving world model for held-out-town transfer. 

D. Structured Representations for Transfer 

Disentangled representation learning seeks factors that correspond to distinct underlying generative causes, with the hope that separating stable content from nuisance variation improves transfer. Domain Separation Networks explicitly split representations into shared and private com-ponents to isolate domain-invariant features from domain-specific ones [23]. InfoGAN introduces an information-theoretic objective to encourage latent variables to capture interpretable factors in generative models [24]. In robotics and RL, DARLA argues that learning disentangled rep-resentations (with unsupervised objectives) before policy learning can improve zero-shot transfer [25]. Further work studies how factorization choices and inductive biases im-pact disentanglement properties [26], and how identifiability constraints can be used to make learned factors more stable and useful under domain shifts [27]. More recently, “disentangled world models” have been proposed to transfer semantic knowledge by separating semantics from other visual variation inside predictive models [28]. Our method is related to this line of work in that it introduces structure into the latent representation, but it does not claim a full semantic–style disentanglement result. Instead, it uses a semantic projection of the deterministic RSSM state and a separate history-conditioned context feature that conditions semantic rollout prediction. The goal is pragmatic: encourage route-relevant semantics while reducing town-predictive information in the semantic branch. 

E. Semantic and Vision–Language Priors for Driving Representations 

Semantic abstraction is a recurring strategy for robust-ness: if the representation emphasizes geometry and agents rather than textures or lighting, policies may generalize bet-ter. A prominent example is using privileged or structured signals in simulation to learn robust policies that deploy with standard sensors [6]. More recently, vision–language models (VLMs) such as CLIP provide large-scale semantic priors learned from image–text pairs, enabling semantically meaningful embeddings that can be reused for downstream tasks [29]. Several robotics and RL works leverage CLIP-like embeddings as supervision or as a mechanism to connect behavior to high-level semantics; RoboCLIP argues that CLIP-aligned representations can reduce demonstra-tion requirements by transferring semantic structure from pretraining [30]. CLIP-derived similarity has also been used as an RL reward signal to encourage goal-aligned behavior, including in driving [31]. Under distribution shifts, there is also interest in adapting frozen VLMs efficiently for domain adaptation settings [32]. Related directions use CLIP as a training signal for temporally grounded motion understanding [33]. We use CLIP [4] differently from reward-shaping ap-proaches. Rather than converting CLIP similarity into re-ward, we use frozen OpenCLIP image embeddings as multi-horizon semantic targets for imagined rollouts, thereby regularizing the predictive model rather than the policy objective directly. 

F. Robustness and Evaluation Protocols in Simulated Driv-ing 

Evaluation methodology matters disproportionately un-der distribution shift: small changes in town layout, traffic patterns, or sensing can produce qualitatively different failure modes. CARLA provides a widely used simulation platform for developing and benchmarking autonomous driving policies [1], and the CARLA Leaderboard and associated challenges formalize standardized tasks and metrics for closed-loop driving evaluation [34]. Earlier, NoCrash evaluated generalization to unseen towns and conditions in CARLA and is a more direct precedent for the present held-out-town setting than generic sim-ulator citations alone [35]. More recently, Bench2Drive and Bench2Drive-R emphasize difficult long-horizon driving scenarios and robustness stress tests to better separate methods that merely fit training distributions from those that generalize [36], [37]. We do not propose a new benchmark or a new planning stack. Instead, this paper combines multi-horizon semantic rollout supervision with town-adversarial regularization in-side a DreamerV3-style route-following agent and evaluates that combination under a focused held-out-town CARLA protocol. 

III. Methodology 

A. Problem Formulation and Setting 

We formulate the task as fixed-route closed-loop naviga-tion over predefined start-goal route instances in CARLA. The reference route is used only by the environment to compute progress, reward, success, and termination; no explicit navigation command, route waypoint, goal pose, or map input is provided to the policy. Each episode instantiates a route defined by a start pose and an end pose, and terminates upon success (reaching the goal) or failure conditions (e.g., collision, persistent offroad/wrong-lane, being stuck, or timeout). [−0.5, −0.25 , −0.1, 0.0, 0.1, 0.25 , 0.5] ,

and the longitudinal command set is 

[−1.0, −0.9, . . . , 0.9, 1.0] .

Positive longitudinal commands are mapped to throttle and negative longitudinal commands are mapped to brake magnitude before applying the resulting control to the simulator. This yields a fixed discrete-action interface shared across all reported methods. No navigation command, route polyline, goal pose, or map input is provided to the agent; all methods use the same route-unconditioned observation interface.

D. Compared Methods and Ablations 

All compared agents are implemented within the same DreamerV3-style world-model reinforcement-learning framework and share the same simulator, route sets, action space, camera configuration, replay pipeline, and episode termination conditions. The comparisons differ only in whether the semantic projection and context branch are enabled, whether the actor and critic consume the standard Dreamer feature ft or the semantic feature st, and which auxiliary losses are active. 

Scope of comparison: The goal of the empirical comparison is controlled mechanism isolation rather than leaderboard-style cross-paradigm ranking. We therefore keep the simulator, fixed route sets, camera observa-tion interface, action discretization, replay pipeline, and DreamerV3-style world-model backbone fixed, and vary only the latent regularization mechanisms under study. Several strong CARLA systems discussed in Section II belong to materially different problem settings, includ-ing command-conditioned imitation policies, privileged-teacher/student pipelines, and planner- or world-model systems evaluated with different conditioning signals, supervision, or benchmark protocols. Evaluating such methods with their original inputs would introduce ad-ditional confounds beyond the proposed auxiliaries, while re-engineering them to fit the present route-unconditioned fixed-route interface would materially change the methods themselves. For this reason, the primary comparison is restricted to matched Dreamer-family variants, and broader cross-paradigm benchmarking is left to future work. 

a) Primary baselines: We use the following primary comparison methods.  

> •

Dreamer-Std : the standard DreamerV3-style agent without RGB augmentation, without visual-semantic supervision, and without domain supervision.  

> •

Dreamer-Aug : the same base Dreamer agent trained with image-space data augmentation only. In the current configuration, this includes random brightness, contrast, and gamma perturbations, Gaussian blur, and additive image noise.  

> •

Dreamer-DANN : the base Dreamer agent with town-domain adversarial supervision applied to the standard Dreamer feature ft through gradient reversal. This baseline uses town labels but does not use the semantic rollout auxiliary branch.  

> •

Sem+Adv-Aux (Ours): semantic projection and context branch are enabled together with seman-tic rollout supervision and semantic-branch town-adversarial supervision. The actor and critic retain the standard Dreamer feature ft, so the semantic/context branch acts as an auxiliary regularizer rather than replacing the base control state. The context-side town-classification loss is disabled. 

b) Targeted ablations: To isolate the contribution of each design choice, we also evaluate the following ablations.  

> •

SemSty-NoLoss: semantic projection and context branch are enabled, but all semantic and domain auxiliary losses are disabled. This tests whether the architectural change alone affects performance.  

> •

Sem-Rollout-Aux: semantic projection and context branch are enabled and the semantic rollout loss is active, but all domain-related losses are disabled. This isolates the effect of visual-semantic future supervision alone.  

> •

Full-Aux: extends Sem+Adv-Aux (Ours) by addi-tionally enabling context-side town classification while keeping the actor and critic on the standard Dreamer feature ft. This tests whether the extra context-side domain objective improves over the primary proposed model.  

> •

Policy-Sem-NoLoss: semantic projection and con-text branch are enabled and the actor/critic consume the semantic feature st directly, but all semantic and domain auxiliary losses are disabled. This tests whether simply changing the policy input to the semantic branch is sufficient.  

> •

Small-GRL Semantic Policy: uses semantic-policy inputs together with semantic rollout supervision, semantic-branch town-adversarial supervision, and context-side town classification, with a weak maximum gradient-reversal strength.  

> •

Large-GRL Semantic Policy: is identical to the previous variant except for a strong maximum gradient-reversal strength. For town-labeled methods, Town05 and Town06 define the source-domain labels. Held-out-town episodes are not used for optimization; if evaluation rollouts are logged, 9

their domain mask is set to 0 so that no domain-related loss is computed. 

E. Training Protocol 

All agents are trained exclusively on source-town expe-rience. In the current multi-town training setup, source data are collected from Town05 and Town06, and the held-out towns are excluded from gradient updates throughout training. For methods that use town supervision, the source-domain labels are defined by the source-town identity, i.e., Town05 and Town06 correspond to the two supervised town classes used during training. All compared methods share the same base DreamerV3-style optimization pipeline, including the same world-model backbone, replay mechanism, imagined-rollout actor–critic updates, camera preprocessing, discrete action interface, and episode termination rules. The methods differ only in the auxiliary components enabled for training: RGB augmentation, semantic projection and context branch, semantic rollout supervision, town-adversarial supervision, and context-side town supervision. This controlled protocol ensures that performance differences are attributable to the proposed regularization mechanisms rather than to unrelated changes in optimizer or simulator configuration. Each method is trained from scratch under three random seeds. In the current training script, the seeds are {2, 3, 4},and each seed corresponds to a separate run with its own log directory and checkpoint history. For methods using semantic rollout supervision, replayed transitions additionally carry frozen OpenCLIP image embeddings. For methods using town supervision, replayed transitions carry the corresponding source-town label and domain mask. Methods that do not use a given auxiliary signal are trained with the corresponding inputs and losses disabled. Training uses the variant definitions from Section IV-D ;the primary proposed method is Sem+Adv-Aux (Ours) .Auxiliary loss weights are fixed across seeds. The semantic-rollout weight is 0.3 in the main auxiliary models, the adversarial town loss weight is 1.0 when enabled, the context-side town-classification weight is either 0 or 1.0 de-pending on the variant, and the maximum gradient-reversal coefficient is set to 0, 0.1, or 1.0 according to the method definition. RGB augmentation is used only in Dreamer-Aug. No target-town data are used for optimization, fine-tuning, or adaptation at any stage. For each seed, model selection used source-only validation episodes sampled from the same Town05 and Town06 route pools used during training; no disjoint source-route validation split was used. Checkpoints were evaluated every 10K training steps on 20 closed-loop source-only validation episodes in total, sampled from the Town05 and Town06 route pools. The checkpoint with the highest mean source-only validation success was selected; ties were broken by lower collision rate. No held-out-town data were used for model selection. Overall, the ablations support a targeted conclusion: the best transfer performance comes from combining semantic rollout supervision with domain-adversarial regularization, rather than from semantic/con-text architecture alone, adversarial regularization alone, or a more heavily loaded auxiliary objective. 

D. Discussion 

Across both held-out towns, the most defensible conclu-sion from the current study is that combining semantic rollout supervision with domain-adversarial latent regular-ization improves zero-shot held-out-town route-following performance more reliably than standard Dreamer training, image augmentation alone, or domain-adversarial training alone. The main result is higher mean held-out-town success within the evaluated protocol, not uniform superiority on every safety or lane-keeping metric. This advantage is expressed most clearly in successful closed-loop route completion on unseen town layouts, while the lane-keeping statistics further suggest improved route adherence on the harder held-out town. At the same time, the proposed method does not dominate every secondary driving metric on every town, so the evidence supports a measured improvement in transfer performance rather than uniform superiority across all operating points. The scope of this conclusion should remain explicit. The present experiments isolate fixed-weather town shift in a controlled CARLA route-following setting and do not test broader sources of distribution shift such as dynamic traffic, pedestrians, or weather variation. Accordingly, the results should be interpreted as evidence for improved held-out-town generalization within this benchmark, not as a general claim of robust autonomous driving under all out-of-distribution conditions. 

E. Limitations and Threats to Validity 

This study has several important limitations. First, the evaluation isolates structural cross-town variation under 14 

fixed ClearNoon weather and without traffic or pedestrians, so the results do not establish robustness to dynamic agents or weather shift. Second, the task is route-unconditioned: the policy receives no navigation command, route polyline, goal pose, or map input, and the reference route is used only by the environment for reward and termination. Third, all methods observe collision and lane-invasion event bits as part of the common observation interface. Finally, the study uses only two source towns and two held-out towns. The findings should therefore be interpreted as evidence for improved transfer within this controlled CARLA benchmark rather than as a general autonomous-driving claim. A further limitation is that the study does not in-clude broader cross-family baselines such as command-conditioned imitation-learning systems or planner-based CARLA pipelines. This omission is intentional and re-flects the question being asked: the present paper tests whether semantic rollout supervision and town-adversarial regularization improve held-out-town transfer within a fixed Dreamer-style route-unconditioned agent, not whether that agent family dominates other driving paradigms that use different conditioning signals, supervision, or task definitions. A broader cross-family study would be valuable, but making it fair would require either harmonizing those additional inputs and supervision across methods or accepting a less controlled comparison with multiple simultaneous confounds. 

VI. Conclusion 

This paper studied zero-shot held-out-town transfer for route-unconditioned fixed-route closed-loop driving in CARLA under a deliberately controlled protocol. Train-ing was performed only on Town05 and Town06, while evaluation was carried out zero-shot on held-out Town03 and Town04 under fixed weather and without traffic or pedestrians. Within a DreamerV3-style world-model agent, we introduced two training-time auxiliary losses: multi-horizon prediction of frozen OpenCLIP image embeddings along imagined rollouts, and town-adversarial supervision on a semantic projection of the deterministic RSSM state. A history-conditioned context feature was used to condition the semantic rollout predictor, while the actor and critic retained the standard Dreamer feature so that the semantic/context branch acted as an auxiliary regularizer rather than replacing the control input. Across both held-out towns, the combined Sem+Adv-Aux model achieved the highest mean route success among the compared Dreamer-family methods. Taken together, the results indicate that combining semantic rollout super-vision with town-adversarial regularization can improve mean held-out-town transfer in this controlled CARLA setting. At the same time, the evidence is deliberately bounded: the gains are not uniform across all secondary safety and lane-keeping metrics, and the proposed model should not be interpreted as dominating every aspect of closed-loop driving performance. The present study should therefore be read as a fo-cused simulation-based analysis rather than as evidence of broad autonomous-driving robustness or as a cross-paradigm CARLA ranking. The experiments do not es-tablish robustness to weather variation, background traffic, pedestrians, sensor corruption, or real-world deployment, and they do not address richer command-conditioned or map-conditioned driving settings. Future work should test whether the same auxiliary design remains useful under broader sources of distribution shift, larger sets of held-out towns and routes, and stronger cross-family baselines evaluated under a carefully harmonized protocol. 15
